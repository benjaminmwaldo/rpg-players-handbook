Chapter IIntroduction

Welcome to a tabletop roleplaying game built from the ground up to deliver tactical, grounded play with constant meaningful choices. This system was designed to fix the pain points of traditional fantasy RPGs while preserving the things that make them great: the thrill of rolling dice, the joy of building a character, and the tension of life-or-death combat.

What Is This Game?

This is a fantasy tabletop RPG for 3–6 players and a Game Master (GM). One player acts as the GM, who describes the world, controls enemies and NPCs, and adjudicates rules. The other players each control a single character—a hero exploring dangerous dungeons, navigating political intrigue, or battling ancient evils.

Design Philosophy

Every rule in this book exists because it was tested and earned its place. The guiding principles are:

  • 5 Stats, Not 6. Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma are collapsed into two stats—Sense and Wit—that are actually distinct and useful. No more confusion about which mental stat to use.
  • 3d6 Bell Curve, Not a d20. Rolling 3d6 produces a predictable bell curve (average 10.5) where skill and stats matter more than luck. A trained warrior consistently outfights a peasant. Wild swings are rare, but when they happen, they feel legendary.
  • No Stat Inflation. Stats start between 9 and 12 and grow slowly. A level 20 hero is formidable, not a demigod. The math stays tight from level 1 to level 20.
  • Every Level Matters. There are no dead levels. Every single level grants a meaningful new ability—not just a number increase, but something that changes how you play.
  • Reactive Combat. You declare reactions after the enemy rolls to hit but before damage is rolled. Every incoming attack is a tactical decision: dodge, block, parry, or take the hit and save your actions?
  • One Resource System. Fighters, Rogues, and Mages all use the same unified resource—Stamina. No tracking separate spell slots, rage charges, and ki points. One pool, three different ways to spend it.
  • Feats Add Options, Not Numbers. Feats give you new things to do, not passive stat bonuses. Your class defines your power; feats define your style.

What You Need to Play

  • 3–5 six-sided dice (d6) per player
  • A character sheet
  • A battle grid with 5-foot squares (for combat)
  • This handbook

Level 0: Your Origin

Every character begins at Level 0—an ordinary person before they take up the adventuring life. At Level 0 you choose your Race, assign your stats, pick a Background, and select your first Minor Feat. You have no class yet; that comes at Level 1 when your character answers the call to adventure.

Level 1: Your Class

At Level 1, you choose one of three classes—Fighter, Rogue, or Mage—and select a Discipline within that class. This defines your combat role and how you spend your Stamina resource. At Level 5, you further specialize by choosing a Subclass.

Leveling

Leveling occurs at the GM's discretion, with no XP system. The GM awards a new level when the story demands it—after a major quest, a dramatic turning point, or when the party has earned it through play. Every level grants a meaningful trait; there are no stat-only levels. You always get something new to do.

Chapter IIIRaces

Your race represents your character's heritage. Each race grants a +1 bonus to one stat, one unique racial trait, and one minor flavor ability. Races are intentionally simple—your class and feats define your power, not your bloodline.

Human

Stat Bonus: +1 to any stat of your choice

Racial Trait — Adaptable: You gain one additional Minor Feat at character creation.

Flavor Ability — Determined: Once per long rest, when you fail a skill check, you may reroll it. You must take the second result.

Elf

Stat Bonus: +1 Sense

Racial Trait — Keen Senses: You gain proficiency in Perception. If you already have it, you gain Minor Advantage on Perception checks.

Flavor Ability — Trance: You need only 4 hours of meditative rest to gain the benefits of a long rest. You remain semi-conscious during this time.

Dwarf

Stat Bonus: +1 Vigor

Racial Trait — Stout: You have Minor Advantage on saving throws against poison, and you take 1 less damage from poison effects (minimum 0).

Flavor Ability — Stonecunning: When examining stonework, you automatically notice unusual construction, hidden seams, or traps built into stone.

Halfling

Stat Bonus: +1 Reflex

Racial Trait — Lucky: When you roll three 1s (critical failure), you may reroll all dice. You must take the new result.

Flavor Ability — Small and Quiet: You can move through the space of any creature one size larger than you. You gain Minor Advantage on Stealth checks when hiding behind larger creatures.

Orc

Stat Bonus: +1 Might

Racial Trait — Relentless: Once per long rest, when damage would reduce you to 0 HP, you drop to 1 HP instead.

Flavor Ability — Menacing: You gain proficiency in Intimidation. If you already have it, you gain Minor Advantage on Intimidation checks.

Tiefling

Stat Bonus: +1 Wit

Racial Trait — Infernal Resistance: You take 2 less damage from fire effects (minimum 0).

Flavor Ability — Darkvision: You can see in dim light as if it were bright light, and in darkness as if it were dim light, out to 60 feet.

Chapter IVStats & Modifiers

Your character is defined by five stats. Unlike systems with six attributes and confusing mental stat overlap, these five stats are distinct and cover everything your character might do.

The Five Stats

MightSTR
ReflexDEX
VigorCON
SensePER
WitINT
  • Might — Raw physical power. Melee damage, carrying capacity, breaking things, grappling. Used for bow damage (capped by draw weight).
  • Reflex — Speed and agility. Dodge rolls, initiative, finesse weapon accuracy, Stealth.
  • Vigor — Toughness and endurance. Hit points, stamina recovery, resisting poison and disease, death threshold.
  • Sense — Awareness, perception, intuition, and social reading. Ranged accuracy, initiative, Perception, Insight. Replaces the Wisdom/Charisma perception overlap.
  • Wit — Intellect, cunning, force of personality, and magical aptitude. Spellcasting, knowledge skills, Persuasion, Deception. Replaces the Intelligence/Charisma overlap.

The Starting Array

Every character starts with the same stat array, distributed however you choose:

12 • 11 • 10 • 10 • 9

After assigning the array, apply your racial +1 bonus. Stats can increase further through leveling.

Stat Modifiers

Your modifier is derived from your stat score. The modifier is what you add to dice rolls.

Modifier Formula

Modifier = Stat − 10

Stat ScoreModifierNotes
7−3Severely deficient
8−2Below average
9−1Slightly below average
10+0Average
11+1Above average
12+2Notably capable
13+3Exceptional
14+4Remarkable
15+5Legendary
16+6Peak mortal ability
Starting Modifier Range

With the starting array (9–12) and a racial +1, new characters have modifiers ranging from −1 to +3. This keeps the starting math tight: on 3d6 (average 10.5), even a +2 swing is significant. By level 20, the highest stats will reach around 16 (+6), keeping the system grounded.

Chapter VBackgrounds

Your background represents what your character did before becoming an adventurer. Each background grants one skill proficiency, a narrative benefit (a roleplaying perk the GM adjudicates), and starting equipment flavor.

Soldier

Proficiency: Athletics

Narrative Benefit — Military Rank: Soldiers and guards recognize your bearing. You can invoke your former rank to gain access to military camps, requisition simple supplies, or command respect from enlisted folk.

Starting Gear Flavor: A rank insignia, a worn weapon maintenance kit, a set of dice.

Scholar

Proficiency: Lore

Narrative Benefit — Researcher: When you encounter information you don't know, you often know where or from whom that knowledge can be found—a library, a sage, an archive.

Starting Gear Flavor: A journal of notes, ink and quill, a letter of introduction from a mentor.

Criminal

Proficiency: Streetwise

Narrative Benefit — Underworld Contact: You have a reliable contact in the criminal world who can fence goods, gather rumors, or arrange meetings with shady figures.

Starting Gear Flavor: A set of lockpicks, dark clothing, a small crowbar.

Artisan

Proficiency: Craft

Narrative Benefit — Guild Member: You belong to a crafting guild. You can find work and lodging in any town with a guild hall, and fellow artisans are generally well-disposed toward you.

Starting Gear Flavor: Artisan's tools of your trade, a guild token, a sample of your best work.

Noble

Proficiency: Persuasion

Narrative Benefit — Position of Privilege: Common folk make way for you, merchants offer better prices, and you can secure an audience with local leaders. Your name carries weight.

Starting Gear Flavor: Fine clothes, a signet ring, a letter of pedigree.

Outlander

Proficiency: Survival

Narrative Benefit — Wanderer: You have an excellent memory for terrain and can always recall the general layout of regions you've traveled. You can forage food and fresh water for yourself and up to five others in the wild.

Starting Gear Flavor: A hunting trap, a trophy from a beast, a hand-drawn map of your homeland.

Acolyte

Proficiency: Medicine

Narrative Benefit — Shelter of the Faithful: You and your companions can receive free healing and care at temples of your faith. Priests will provide what aid they can, short of endangering themselves.

Starting Gear Flavor: A holy symbol, a prayer book, vestments, and incense.

Merchant

Proficiency: Appraisal

Narrative Benefit — Trade Network: You know the trade routes and market conditions in major settlements. You can usually find a buyer for unusual goods and negotiate 10% better prices when selling.

Starting Gear Flavor: A set of fine scales, a ledger, a letter of credit from a trading house.

Chapter VIClasses & Disciplines

At Level 1, you choose one of three classes. Each class has a fundamentally different relationship with the Stamina resource and the action economy. Within each class, you choose a Discipline—a fighting style that grants passive bonuses when its conditions are met.

The Three Pillars
  • Fighter — Controls space through combat modifiers. Spends Stamina on empowering attacks, blocks, and positioning.
  • Rogue — Creates openings through exploitation. Spends Stamina on modifying attacks, dodges, and blocks to expose enemies and exploit weakness.
  • Mage — Shapes reality through spell construction. Spends Stamina to forge and modify spells for a variety of outcomes.

Fighter

Fighters are masters of martial combat who control the battlefield through superior technique. They use Stamina to empower their attacks, fortify their defenses, and manipulate positioning. A Fighter's strength is consistency—they are always dangerous, always reliable, and always in control of the space around them.

Hit Points: 10 + Vigor modifier per level
Guard (Base): Determined by armor
Stamina Pool: 4 + Vigor modifier (recovers on rest)
Proficiencies: All simple weapons, light and medium armor, shields

Fighter Disciplines

Two-Handed Fighting

When wielding a two-handed weapon:

  • +1 to Block actions
  • When you roll for damage, reroll any 1s on damage dice

Duelist

When wielding at least one single-handed weapon and no shield:

  • +1 passive Guard against any enemies you hit on your last turn
  • +1 damage against isolated enemies (enemies with no adjacent allies)

Shield

When wielding a shield:

  • +1 passive Guard
  • If you take the Block action and are still hit by 1, reduce the damage by 2

Archer

Ranged combat specialist:

  • You can load an arrow when you equip the bow (saves an action)
  • +1 accuracy with the bow when no enemies are adjacent to you

Rogue

Rogues are opportunists who thrive on exploiting every weakness. They use Stamina to modify their attacks, dodges, and blocks—turning every exchange into a chance to expose the enemy for a devastating follow-up. Surprised enemies are Exposed, giving Rogues the highest single-target burst damage in the game.

Hit Points: 8 + Vigor modifier per level
Guard (Base): Determined by armor
Stamina Pool: 4 + Reflex modifier (recovers on rest)
Proficiencies: All simple weapons, finesse weapons, light armor

Rogue Disciplines

Ambusher

  • +1 initiative
  • +2 to your first damage roll every combat

Predator

  • When attacking a target with no adjacent allies, increase your Exposed bonus by +1
  • If you successfully Dodge an isolated target, your next attack against them treats them as Exposed

Evasionist

  • +1 to your first Dodge each round
  • Once per turn, after you successfully Dodge, you may move 5 feet
  • Once per turn, after you successfully Dodge, gain +2 Guard until the start of your next turn

Mage

Mages shape raw magical energy into powerful effects using spell construction. They choose a magical Form (school) and build spells on the fly by combining Base effects with Modifiers. Every spell is a resource decision—more powerful effects cost more Stamina.

Hit Points: 6 + Vigor modifier per level
Guard (Base): Determined by armor
Stamina Pool: 6 + Wit modifier (recovers on rest)
Proficiencies: Simple weapons, no armor (unless gained via feats)

Mage Disciplines

Ritual Caster

Patient and methodical:

  • Taking an extra action to cast a spell decreases the Stamina cost (stacking) and increases effectiveness

Non-Combat Caster

Utility specialist:

  • When casting a spell that has no damage effect, the Stamina cost is reduced by 1 (minimum 1)

Complex Caster

Master of layered magic:

  • The third modifier added to a spell costs significantly less Stamina

Chapter VIISubclasses

At Level 5, you choose a Subclass that further defines your role. Subclasses blend the strengths of two class archetypes and unlock powerful new abilities at levels 5, 9, 13, and 17.

SubclassArchetype BlendRole
WarmasterFighterBattlefield commander, ally buffs, tactical positioning
AssassinRogueBurst damage, poison, stealth kills
ArcanistMageRaw spell power, metamagic-style enhancements
SkirmisherFighter / RogueMobility, hit-and-run, fluid positioning
SpellbladeFighter / MageWeapon-channeled magic, melee-magic hybrid
TricksterRogue / MageIllusions, debuffs, misdirection

Warmaster

Fighter Subclass — The Battlefield Commander

Warmasters lead from the front. They empower allies, control positioning, and turn every engagement into a coordinated assault.

Level 5 — Commanding Presence

Once per round, when an ally within 30 feet makes an attack, you can spend 1 Stamina as a free action to grant them +1 to hit.

Level 9 — Tactical Repositioning

When you take the Attack action, you may spend 1 Stamina to allow one ally within 30 feet to immediately move 10 feet without provoking reactions.

Level 13 — Rallying Strike

When you score a critical success on an attack, all allies within 30 feet recover 1 Stamina.

Level 17 — Supreme Commander

At the start of each round, choose one ally within 30 feet. That ally gains +1 Guard and +1 to their next damage roll until the start of your next turn. This does not cost Stamina.

Assassin

Rogue Subclass — The Silent Killer

Assassins specialize in ending fights before they begin. Poison, ambush, and overwhelming burst damage are their tools.

Level 5 — Death Strike

When you hit an Exposed or surprised enemy, you deal an additional 1d6 damage. This stacks with other Exposed bonuses.

Level 9 — Poison Craft

During a short rest, you can prepare a number of poison doses equal to your Wit modifier (minimum 1). A poisoned weapon deals +2 damage on its next hit and inflicts the Poisoned condition. Each dose lasts until used or until your next long rest.

Level 13 — Vanish

You can spend 2 Stamina to become hidden as a free action on your turn, even in combat, as long as you are not adjacent to an enemy. Enemies must succeed on a Sense check vs. your Stealth to target you.

Level 17 — Assassinate

When you hit a surprised enemy, your damage dice are doubled. If this reduces the target to 0 HP, you regain 2 Stamina.

Arcanist

Mage Subclass — The Master of Raw Power

Arcanists push the boundaries of spell construction, layering more modifiers and amplifying their effects beyond what other casters can achieve.

Level 5 — Arcane Surge

Once per combat, when you cast a spell, you may add one modifier at no Stamina cost.

Level 9 — Spell Shaping

When you cast an area spell, you can exclude a number of targets equal to your Wit modifier from the effect.

Level 13 — Overcharge

You may spend 2 additional Stamina when casting a spell to increase all damage dice by one size (d6 becomes d8, d8 becomes d10, etc.).

Level 17 — Arcane Mastery

You learn a second magical Form. You can construct spells from either Form and may even combine Base effects from both in a single spell (at +3 Stamina cost).

Skirmisher

Fighter / Rogue Hybrid — The Mobile Striker

Skirmishers refuse to stand still. They weave in and out of combat, striking where defenses are weakest and never staying long enough to be pinned down.

Level 5 — Hit and Run

After you make a melee attack, you may move 5 feet without provoking reactions. This movement does not cost an action.

Level 9 — Exploit Momentum

If you move at least 15 feet before making an attack, you deal +2 damage on that attack.

Level 13 — Elusive

Attacks of opportunity against you have Minor Disadvantage. Additionally, you cannot be grappled while you have movement remaining.

Level 17 — Whirlwind

Once per combat, you may spend 3 Stamina to make one melee attack against every enemy you move adjacent to during your turn (maximum 4 attacks). You must move at least 5 feet between each attack.

Spellblade

Fighter / Mage Hybrid — The Arcane Warrior

Spellblades channel magic through their weapons, combining martial skill with arcane power into a devastating hybrid combat style.

Level 5 — Arcane Weapon

You can spend 1 Stamina to infuse your weapon with magical energy. Your next melee attack deals +1d6 elemental damage (choose fire, frost, or lightning when you infuse).

Level 9 — Spell Strike

When you make a melee attack, you may simultaneously cast a spell with a single target, delivering it through the weapon. Both use the same hit roll. The spell costs its normal Stamina.

Level 13 — Arcane Shield

When you take the Block or Dodge reaction, you may spend 1 Stamina to add your Wit modifier to the roll.

Level 17 — Eldritch Fury

When you score a critical success on a melee attack, you may immediately cast a spell as a free action at half Stamina cost (rounded up).

Trickster

Rogue / Mage Hybrid — The Master of Misdirection

Tricksters blend illusion magic with roguish cunning, controlling the battlefield through deception, debuffs, and misdirection.

Level 5 — Illusory Double

You can spend 1 Stamina to create an illusory duplicate of yourself within 10 feet. The duplicate lasts until the end of your next turn or until hit. Enemies must succeed on a Sense check (TN 10 + your Wit modifier) or target the duplicate instead of you.

Level 9 — Hexing Strike

When you hit an Exposed enemy, you may spend 1 Stamina to inflict one condition of your choice: Blinded, Frightened, or Silenced. The condition lasts until the end of your next turn.

Level 13 — Misdirection

When you are targeted by an attack, you may spend 2 Stamina as a reaction to redirect the attack to another creature within 5 feet of you (ally or enemy). The attacker rerolls against the new target's Guard.

Level 17 — Grand Illusion

Once per long rest, you can spend 4 Stamina to create a large-scale illusion (up to 30-foot cube) that affects all senses. Creatures within must pass a Wit check (TN 10 + your Wit modifier + proficiency) each turn or believe it is real. The illusion lasts up to 1 minute.

Chapter VIIIFeats

Feats are special abilities that let you customize your character beyond their class. There are two types:

  • Minor Feats: Small, flavorful perks for differentiation. You gain one at Level 0.
  • Major Feats: Combat-defining abilities that unlock new mechanics. Gained through leveling.

Feats are intentionally not class-specific. Your class and subclass define your core power; feats define your personal style.

Minor Feats

Actor
You can mimic accents and the voices of those you are familiar with. You gain Minor Advantage on Deception checks when maintaining a disguise that involves speaking.

Alert
You gain +1 to initiative. During surprise rounds, you act at the lowest initiative (rather than being unable to act).

Athlete
Standing from prone costs no extra movement. You climb and swim at your full movement speed instead of half.

Close-Quarter Marksman
Drawing a bow within the reach of an enemy no longer triggers a reaction opportunity. You can strike an enemy with your bow as a melee weapon, dealing 1d4 + Might bludgeoning damage (better than an unarmed attack).

Lightly Armored
You gain proficiency with light armor.

Moderately Armored
You gain proficiency with medium armor. Prerequisite: Light armor proficiency.

Heavily Armored
You gain proficiency with heavy armor. Prerequisite: Medium armor proficiency.

Dual Wielding
You gain proficiency with dual wielding. You can wield two light weapons simultaneously.

Grappler
You gain +2 on checks to initiate or maintain a grapple. While grappling a creature, you can use 1 action to deal 1d4 + Might damage to them.

Slasher
You gain proficiency with martial slashing weapons.

Bludgeoner
You gain proficiency with martial bludgeoning weapons.

Piercer
You gain proficiency with martial piercing weapons.

Shielder
You gain proficiency with shields. While wielding a shield, you gain +1 Guard.

Duelist
When wielding a single one-handed weapon and nothing in the other hand, you gain +1 damage with that weapon.

Major Feats

Dual Wield Master
When dual wielding, you can take the Block action as if you had a shield. Additionally, when you take the Attack action with your main weapon, you can make a bonus attack with your off-hand weapon for 1 Stamina (deals weapon damage without stat modifier).
Prerequisite: Dual Wielding feat.

Light Armor Master
While wearing light armor, you gain +1 Guard. Additionally, you do not have disadvantage on Stealth checks from armor.
Prerequisite: Light armor proficiency.

Medium Armor Master
While wearing medium armor, the maximum Reflex bonus to Guard increases by +1 (from +2 to +3). Additionally, medium armor no longer imposes disadvantage on Stealth checks.
Prerequisite: Medium armor proficiency.

Heavy Armor Master
While wearing heavy armor, you reduce all non-magical physical damage by 1 (minimum 0). You also reduce knockback effects by 5 feet.
Prerequisite: Heavy armor proficiency.

Slashing Master
When you score a critical success with a slashing weapon, the target begins Bleeding: they take 1d4 damage at the start of each of their turns for 2 rounds. When you hit with a slashing weapon, reduce the target's Guard by 1 until the end of your next turn.
Prerequisite: Martial slashing proficiency.

Bludgeoning Master
When you score a critical success with a bludgeoning weapon, the target must make a Vigor check (TN 12) or be Stunned until the end of their next turn. Normal hits with bludgeoning weapons push the target 5 feet.
Prerequisite: Martial bludgeoning proficiency.

Piercing Master
When you score a critical success with a piercing weapon, you deal an additional 1d6 damage. Your piercing attacks ignore 1 point of damage reduction from armor.
Prerequisite: Martial piercing proficiency.

Shielding Master
When you successfully block an attack, you can push the enemy back 5 feet. You gain +1 to Block actions against projectiles.
Prerequisite: Shield proficiency.

Light Bow Master
You can fire a light bow as a single action (without needing a separate draw action). Your light bow attacks do not provoke reactions when fired within melee range.
Prerequisite: Proficiency with ranged weapons.

Heavy Bow Master
You can add +2 to damage with a heavy bow (beyond the normal draw weight cap) at the cost of −1 to hit. When you spend an extra action aiming, you gain Minor Advantage on the attack.
Prerequisite: Proficiency with ranged weapons.

Great Weapon Master
When you score a critical success with a two-handed weapon, you may make an additional attack as a free action. You can choose to take −1 to hit to deal +3 damage on a two-handed weapon attack.
Prerequisite: Proficiency with martial weapons.

Dueling Master
While wielding a single one-handed weapon and nothing in the other hand, you gain +1 to hit in addition to the Duelist damage bonus. When an enemy misses you with a melee attack, you may spend 1 Stamina to make an immediate counterattack as a reaction.
Prerequisite: Duelist feat.

Chapter IXSkills & Proficiencies

Proficiency

Proficiency represents training and experience. When you are proficient in a weapon, armor type, skill, or tool, you add your Proficiency Bonus of +2 to relevant rolls.

Proficiency Bonus

The proficiency bonus is a flat +2 that does not scale with level. On a 3d6 bell curve, +2 is a significant and consistent advantage. Scaling proficiency would break the tight math this system is built on.

Using a weapon or armor without proficiency: Attack rolls and relevant checks are made with Minor Disadvantage. For armor, your Guard bonus is reduced by 1 and you have Minor Disadvantage on all Reflex-based checks.

Bow Mechanics

Bows use Sense for accuracy (to-hit) and Might for damage, but your Might bonus to damage is capped by the bow's Draw Weight.

  • A player with +4 Might using a Draw Weight 2 bow adds only +2 bonus damage.
  • A player with +1 Might using a Draw Weight 4 bow adds only +1 bonus damage.

Bows take 1 action to draw and 1 action to fire. Drawing a bow within the reach of an enemy triggers a reaction opportunity against you (unless you have the Close-Quarter Marksman feat).

Range: The first number is normal range; the second is long range. Attacks at long range have Minor Disadvantage.

Actions & the Action Economy

Each character has 2 Actions per turn. Actions can be spent on:

  • Attack — Make one melee or ranged attack.
  • Move — Move up to your movement speed (default 30 ft). You can split movement around other actions.
  • Dodge — Prepare to evade. Used as a reaction when attacked.
  • Block — Brace to reduce damage. Used as a reaction when attacked.
  • Use an Item — Draw a weapon, drink a potion, interact with an object.
  • Cast a Spell — Cast a spell (Mages; costs vary).
  • Grapple — Attempt to grab a creature (contested Might check).
  • Shove — Push a creature 5 feet or knock them prone (contested Might check).
  • Stabilize — Attempt to stabilize a downed ally (costs 2 actions).
  • Hold Action — Reserve an action for a reaction.

Reactions

Reaction Timing

Reactions are declared after an enemy rolls to hit but before damage is rolled. This is the core tactical decision of combat: every incoming attack forces you to decide whether to spend an action defending or save it for your turn.

Reactions consume actions from your next turn. If you use both actions as reactions, you have no actions on your next turn.

Reaction Types

ReactionEffectCost
DodgeAdd Reflex modifier to your Guard against this attack1 Action
BlockIf hit, reduce damage by your Block value (shield or weapon-based)1 Action
Parry/CounterClass or weapon-specific. May allow a counterattack or impose penalties.1 Action
Reaction SpellCast a defensive spell (if available and prepared).1 Action + Stamina

Initiative

At the start of combat, each character rolls:

Initiative = 3d6 + Reflex Modifier + Sense Modifier + other bonuses

Highest initiative acts first. Ties are broken by higher Reflex, then higher Sense.

Movement & Grid

  • Combat takes place on a 5-foot battle grid.
  • Standard movement speed is 30 feet per action spent on movement.
  • Moving out of a creature's melee reach provokes a reaction opportunity from that creature.
  • Difficult terrain costs double movement (10 feet per 5 feet traversed).

Flanking

When you and an ally are on opposite sides of an enemy (forming a line through the enemy's space), you both gain +1 to hit that enemy.

Surprise Rounds

When either you surprise an enemy or an enemy surprises you:

  1. All non-surprised parties roll for initiative and take a full round of actions.
  2. Then all combatants roll for initiative, and combat proceeds normally from the top.

Surprised creatures cannot take reactions during the surprise round. Enemies targeted during a surprise round are treated as Exposed.

Exposed Condition

An Exposed creature has dropped their guard. Attacks against an Exposed target deal +2 bonus damage. This is the primary damage amplifier for Rogues.

Chapter XIIMagic System

Magic in this system is built on spell construction. Rather than memorizing a fixed list of spells, Mages choose a magical Form (school of magic) and learn to construct spells by combining Base effects with Modifiers. Every spell costs Stamina, and more powerful combinations cost more.

How Spell Construction Works

  1. Choose a Base effect from your Form. This is the spell's core function.
  2. Add Modifiers (optional). Each modifier enhances or changes the spell, but costs additional Stamina.
  3. Pay the Stamina cost (Base cost + Modifier costs).
  4. Roll to cast: 3d6 + Wit Modifier + Proficiency vs. Target Number (for attack spells, vs. target's Guard).
Spell Construction Cost

Total Stamina Cost = Base Cost + Modifier 1 Cost + Modifier 2 Cost + ...

Players can only learn spells from their chosen Form. Some spells can be cast as Reactions. Mage discipline features can reduce modifier costs.

Chapter XIIIConditions & Status Effects

Conditions represent temporary impairments or states. A creature can have multiple conditions simultaneously. Unless noted, conditions end when their source ends or after the specified duration.

ConditionEffect
ExposedThe creature has dropped its guard. Attacks against it deal +2 bonus damage. Ends at the start of the creature's next turn (unless reapplied).
ProneThe creature is on the ground. Melee attacks against it gain +1 to hit. Ranged attacks against it have −1 to hit. Standing costs 15 ft of movement.
GrappledThe creature's speed is 0. It cannot move away from the grappler. It can still attack and take actions normally. Escape requires a contested Might or Reflex check.
StunnedThe creature loses 1 action on its next turn. It cannot take reactions. Attacks against it have Minor Advantage.
BlindedThe creature cannot see. Attacks it makes have Minor Disadvantage. Attacks against it have Minor Advantage. It automatically fails any check requiring sight.
FrightenedThe creature has Minor Disadvantage on attack rolls and skill checks while the source of fear is within line of sight. It cannot willingly move closer to the source.
PoisonedThe creature has Minor Disadvantage on attack rolls and skill checks. At the start of each of its turns, it takes 1d4 poison damage. Lasts 3 rounds or until cured.
BurningThe creature is on fire. At the start of each of its turns, it takes 1d4 fire damage. It can spend 1 action to extinguish the flames. Lasts until extinguished or 2 rounds.
FrozenThe creature's speed is halved. It has Minor Disadvantage on Reflex-based checks and saves. Lasts 2 rounds.

Chapter XIVHealth, Death & Recovery

Hit Points

Your HP is determined by your class and Vigor modifier:

ClassHP per Level
Fighter10 + Vigor modifier
Rogue8 + Vigor modifier
Mage6 + Vigor modifier

Flinch

Massive hits can stagger even the toughest warriors.

Quarter-HP Flinch

If you take more than ¼ of your max HP in a single hit, you must make a Vigor saving throw (TN 12). On failure, you lose 1 action from your next turn.

Half-HP Flinch

If you take more than ½ of your max HP in a single hit, you must make a Vigor saving throw (TN 15). On failure, you lose 2 actions from your next turn (you are effectively staggered).

Going Down

At 0 HP, you fall unconscious. While unconscious:

  • You lose 2 HP per turn (bleeding out).
  • Death occurs when you reach negative (Vigor score + Vigor modifier) HP.
  • You drop whatever you are holding and fall Prone.

Stabilization

  • Anyone can attempt to stabilize a downed creature by spending 2 Actions.
  • Roll 3d6 + Proficiency vs. TN 12.
  • Relevant proficiencies (Medicine, Healer's Kit) grant Minor Advantage.
  • On success: the creature stabilizes at 0 HP and stops losing HP. They remain unconscious.

Regaining Consciousness

  • A stabilized creature can attempt to wake each round.
  • Roll 3d6 + Vigor modifier vs. TN 14.
  • On success: awaken at 0 HP. You can act normally on your next turn but are vulnerable.
  • On failure: remain unconscious. Try again next round.

Rest & Recovery

Short Rest

A short rest takes approximately 1 hour of downtime—catching your breath, binding wounds, eating rations.

  • Recover HP equal to your Vigor score.
  • Recover half your maximum Stamina (rounded up).

Long Rest

A long rest requires 8 hours of sleep or equivalent rest (4 hours for Elves).

  • Recover all HP.
  • Recover all Stamina.
  • Remove most non-permanent conditions.

Resource System: Stamina

Stamina

Stamina is the universal resource for all classes. Your starting pool depends on your class:

  • Fighter: 4 + Vigor modifier
  • Rogue: 4 + Reflex modifier
  • Mage: 6 + Wit modifier

Stamina grows by +1 at designated levels (see table above). It recovers on rest.
